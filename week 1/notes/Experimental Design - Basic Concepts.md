# Experimental Design: Basic Concepts

## Topics:
	Focus and nuisance variables
	Direct control: constants and treatments
	Indirect control: randomization
	Within-subject vs. between-subject design
	Factorial design

## Focus and Nuisance Variables
- Focus variables: you are interested in the effects of these variables
	- E.g., status quo and new mechanism 
    - Original eBay reputation system: Buyer/Seller to rate transaction either positive/negative/neutal
    - New mechanism: Detailed seller ratings
        - Reason: Based on empirical data, the reviews were too positive
        - Features: anonymous, item as described, communication, shipping time, charges.
        - Control: classic reputation system
        - Treatmemt: classic system + detailed seller ratings
- Nuisance variables: little or no direct interests, but might affect results
    - E.g., subject's alertness and interests

## Direct Control: Constants and Treatments

- Constants: hold a variable constant at a convenient level
- Treatments (experimental conditions): choose a variable at two or more different levels that may produce sharply different outcomes
- Principle: vary all treatment variables independently to obtain the clearest possible evidence on their effects

## Indirect Control: Randomization
- Randomization: assign chosen levels of the treatment variables in random order (randomize roles within each session)
	- Controlled nuisances can cause inferential errors if confounded with focus variables:
		- E.g., new seeds, good weather
			--> improvement in harvests
			
## Between-subject vs. Within-subject Design

---
## Between-subject design
- Between-subject design: vary levels of the focus variable only across subjects
	- Clean
	- Always use it when learning is important
- each subject only experience one experimental condition
	
## Within-subject design: each subject participate in multiple treatments
- When individual idiosyncrasies are important
- Random block: AB, BA
- Cross-over design: ABA, BAB
  - order needs to be controlled

## Latin Square: order effects
- Two or more treatments are contrasted on the same experimental unit
	- Example: testing four tire brands (A, B, C, D) using four test cars:
    - Car 1: A B C D
	- Car 2: C D A B
	- Car 3: D C B A
	- Car 4: B A D C 
	
## Factorial Design
- Two or more treatment variables
	- Goal; No Goal
	- Link; No Link
- When you have two or more treatment variables and you want to set each one of these treatment variables at different levels.
