# What are experiments?
## Better Control 

- The experimenter can vary certain variables in exogenous variable, while keeping everything else constant.
	- Experimenter knows whether variables are exogenous or endogenous
	- Isolation of possible causal relationships
- Exogenous variable: a variation that the study participants cannot control (i.e., unexpected rainfall)
- Endogenous variable: something a decision maker can control (i.e., to drive for Uber or not, for how long to drive for Uber)
	
- Experimenter controls the info conditions (how much info to release to participants)
	- Important for games with asymmetric info
	- Important for replication studies
	
- Participants are randomly assigned to conditions
	- No selection bias
	- Needs simple econometrics (often better than complicated econometric models)
	
- The experimenter can observe variables that are often not observable in the field
	- Examples: on eBay, you can't observe maximum willingness to pay, but in a lab experiment you can.
	
- More control means also that the researcher can calculate the predicted equilibrium exactly
	- Observation of equilibrium and non-equilibrium behavior
	- Observation of fast and slow equilibrium approaches (equilibrium dynamics)
	
## Cause and Effect - A causes B

- The two variables co-vary: correlation
- The covariation between the two variables is not spurious, or not random
- There is a logical time order: A must proceed B in time
- Theory: A mechanism is available that explains how an independent variable causes a dependent variable 

## Correlation is not Causation

- The two variables co-vary: correlation
- Correlation is a necessary, but not sufficient condition for causation
- Warning: Correlation does not equal Causation!
  - Examples:
      - Countries with a high rate of infant diarrhea also have bad road conditions
	
## Spuriousness

- The covariation between the two variables is not spurious
- Example: 
	- Ice cream sales are highest in the city when the rate of drowning in city swimming pools is highest.
	- High temps is the common variable.
	
## Logical time order

- There is a logical time order: A must proceed B in time
  - Examples: 
  	- I kicked the table and hurt my toe.
	
## Role of Theory:

- Theory: A mechanism is available that explains how an independent variable causes a dependent variable

## Role of Theory:
- Theory explains facts 
- Theory make (precise!) predictions
- Theory formulizes intuition
	
## Confounders: Definitions

- Confounders, or lurking variables

  - Definition 1: a variable which influences both who is selected for the treatment and the outcome of the experiment
  - Definition 2: a variable that influences both the dependent variable and independent variable, causing a spurious association
	
## Confounders: Example

- Do SAT preparation courses (x) improve SAT scores (y)?
  - Correlation: Yes
  - What are unobserved factors that predict SAT scores and are correlated with taking a prep course?
      - Family wealth (z) (positively correlated with both x and y)
		
- Control group: no test prep
- Treatment group: test prep
- Random Assignment: randomly assign participants to either group (to receive test prep or not) to resolve the issue of confounding
