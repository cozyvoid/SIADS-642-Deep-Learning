## Purpose of the Chapter

This chapter introduces **randomized evaluations** (randomized
controlled trials, or RCTs) as a rigorous method for determining whether
a program actually causes measurable improvements in outcomes.

It demonstrates: - Why randomized evaluations are powerful tools for
causal inference - How a randomized evaluation is designed and
implemented in practice - How evidence from such evaluations can
influence policy and scale programs globally

The chapter contains two major sections:

1.  **Module 1.1 -- The Power of Randomized Evaluations**
2.  **Module 1.2 -- A Randomized Evaluation from Start to Finish**

## Module 1.1: The Power of Randomized Evaluations

## Core Idea

Randomized evaluations provide **credible evidence of causal impact**,
allowing researchers and policymakers to determine whether a program
truly works.

They solve the fundamental challenge of evaluation:

> distinguishing the impact of a program from other factors affecting
> outcomes.

Without randomization, improvements might be incorrectly attributed to a
program when they are actually caused by other factors.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Case Study: Deworming Program in Kenya

The chapter begins with a real-world example involving **International
Child Support (ICS) Africa**, an NGO supporting Kenyan schools.

Researchers suggested randomly phasing in programs across schools to
evaluate their effects.

### Programs Tested

-   Providing textbooks
-   Constructing classrooms
-   Hiring additional teachers
-   Reducing schooling costs (free uniforms, meals)
-   Incentives for teachers or students
-   Health interventions such as **mass deworming**

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Results of the Deworming Evaluation

The randomized evaluation found:

-   Deworming reduced school absenteeism by **25%**
-   It was one of the **most cost‑effective education interventions**

### Long‑term effects

Children who were dewormed experienced:

-   Increased education among girls
-   Men working **3.5 additional hours per week**
-   Higher wages and better employment outcomes

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Policy Impact

Because of strong causal evidence:

-   **2009:** Kenya launched a national deworming program for **3
    million children**
-   **2013:** Global programs expanded to **40 million children**

This illustrates how **small evaluations can influence policies
affecting millions of people.**

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Expansion of Randomized Evaluations

Historically:

-   RCTs were mainly conducted in rich countries
-   They were extremely expensive

Partnerships between **researchers and NGOs** showed that high‑quality
randomized evaluations could be conducted:

-   in low‑income settings
-   with limited budgets
-   while still maintaining methodological rigor

Researchers developed new techniques to address challenges such as:

-   spillover effects
-   attrition
-   measuring complex outcomes
-   maximizing statistical power with small samples

## Module 1.2: A Randomized Evaluation from Start to Finish

This section walks through a **full evaluation conducted in India** to
illustrate how randomized evaluations are implemented.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### 1. Choosing the Right Question

Researchers at MIT and **J‑PAL (Abdul Latif Jameel Poverty Action Lab)**
focused on an important policy question:

### Do community accountability programs improve public services?

The theory suggested that when communities monitor schools or clinics,
service quality should improve.

However, most existing evidence was **correlational**, not causal.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Correlation vs Causation

Communities with strong participation may also differ in:

-   education levels
-   wealth
-   governance structures

Thus, better outcomes may not be caused by participation itself.

Randomized evaluation is required to **isolate the causal effect.**

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 2. Selecting the Context and Partner

Researchers partnered with **Pratham**, India's largest education NGO.

### Why Pratham?

-   Extensive education expertise
-   Prior collaboration with researchers
-   Capacity to scale successful programs nationally

### Why India?

Education quality was extremely poor:

-   **25% teacher absenteeism**
-   Only **45% of teachers present were teaching**
-   Many students could not read basic text by grade 3--4

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 3. Understanding Local Context

Researchers conducted extensive fieldwork to learn about:

-   learning levels
-   school governance
-   community awareness

Key discoveries:

-   Learning outcomes were extremely poor
-   Communities overestimated student performance
-   Village Education Committees (VECs) existed but were inactive
-   Many committee members were unaware they were members

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 4. Designing the Program

Researchers and Pratham collaborated for **one year** to design a
scalable intervention.

Goals:

-   low cost
-   replicable
-   appropriate for rural contexts

Field research included interviews with:

-   teachers
-   village leaders
-   parents
-   government officials

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 5. Interventions Tested

Three interventions were developed.

## Intervention 1: Information Campaign

-   Community meetings
-   Information about school resources
-   Training VEC members

Goal: increase community oversight.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Intervention 2: Community Scorecards

Communities tested children's reading ability and produced **village
report cards**.

Goal: motivate action by revealing poor learning outcomes.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Intervention 3: Read India Volunteer Camps

Volunteers were trained to run **after‑school reading camps** for
struggling students.

Goal: provide a direct way for communities to improve learning.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 6. Pilot Study

A pilot program was conducted to:

-   test feasibility
-   refine interventions
-   design surveys
-   estimate required sample size

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# 7. Random Assignment

The study involved **280 villages** randomly assigned to four groups:

  Group         Intervention
  ------------ ------------------------------------------
   Control        No program                                
   Treatment 1    Information                                
   Treatment 2    Information + Scorecards                   
   Treatment 3    Information + Scorecards + Reading Camps  

Randomization occurred **at the village level**.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 8. Theory of Change

Researchers mapped the pathway through which interventions could improve
learning.

### Inputs

-   information campaigns
-   reading assessments
-   volunteer training

### Intermediate outcomes

-   increased knowledge
-   parental involvement
-   community monitoring

### Final outcomes

-   better school performance
-   improved literacy levels

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 9. Data Collection

Researchers collected data on:

### Schools

-   teacher attendance
-   school functioning

### Households

-   parental awareness
-   educational involvement

### Students

-   literacy and numeracy tests

### Community institutions

-   awareness of VEC roles

Both **baseline and endline surveys** were conducted.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 10. Power Analysis and Sample Size

Power analysis determined the necessary sample size.

Final dataset included:

-   **2,800 households**
-   **316 schools**
-   **17,533 children tested**
-   **1,029 VEC members surveyed**

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 11. Implementation

Interventions were implemented between **September and December 2005**.

Monitoring ensured:

-   proper randomization
-   community meetings occurred
-   reading camps operated correctly

About **7,453 children attended reading camps**.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 12. Data Analysis

Researchers compared outcomes between treatment and control villages.

Key statistical practices included:

-   clustering standard errors
-   grouping outcomes to avoid data mining
-   instrumental variables to estimate treatment effects among camp
    participants

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 13. Results

## Information & Scorecard Interventions

Results:

-   small improvements in awareness
-   **no increase in community involvement**
-   **no improvement in learning outcomes**

Conclusion: information alone did not change behavior.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Read India Camps

Results:

-   significant literacy improvements for participating students

Examples:

-   **1.7% increase** in letter recognition overall
-   **7.9% increase** among children previously unable to recognize
    letters

After adjusting for attendance:

-   camps increased letter recognition by **\~60% among participants**

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 14. Policy Implications

Findings suggested:

-   community oversight alone may not reform institutions
-   people may prefer **direct solutions** rather than bureaucratic
    advocacy

Providing **tools for action** proved more effective than providing
information alone.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 15. Scaling the Program

Following the evaluation:

-   Read India expanded nationwide
-   The program eventually reached **23+ million children**
-   The reading assessment tool became part of India's **Annual Status
    of Education Report (ASER)**

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 16. Lessons About Community Accountability

Evidence from multiple studies shows:

Community participation **can improve services**, but success depends
heavily on context.

Examples:

-   Community monitoring improved health services in Uganda
-   School committees in Kenya had little impact without additional
    resources
-   External audits reduced corruption in Indonesia

Conclusion:

> The effectiveness of participation programs depends on institutional
> details and program design.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Key Takeaways

1.  Randomized evaluations provide strong causal evidence.
2.  Small experiments can influence large-scale policies.
3.  Evaluation design requires strong partnerships and contextual
    understanding.
4.  Information alone rarely changes behavior.
5.  Interventions must provide **actionable solutions**.
6.  Evidence-based policymaking requires continuous experimentation and
    learning.
